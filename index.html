Hello! I am Wyatt.

Cybersecurity student at the Rochester Institute of Technology

I am a fourth-year student in the M.S. Computing Security program at RIT, just starting my career in the industry and striving to create secure, inclusive, accessible, and joyful technologies for all to use. My research interests are in systems and network analytics for malware detection, steganography, and disinformation.

I frequently participate in cybersecurity red/blue competitions and Capture the Flag events, participate in STEM outreach around the world, and, more recently, have been working to address media bias and ethical research issues plaguing the national dialogue. I also enjoy being a lifelong student by incorporating other disciplines and perspectives into my activities, from technology to psychology, philosophy, journalism, communication, and political science.

I am a member of the RIT Security Club (RITSEC), RIT FIRST, and the RIT Honors Program in my spare time. I also enjoy adventurous activities such as geocaching in the Rochester area, bouldering, and traveling with my friends. In the future, I hope to obtain a Ph.D. in information assurance and own a paramotor.

Projects

A selection of projects and activities I've worked on

Malware Meta-Analysis

May 2020

I chose to re-evaluate the methodologies and rigor of academic malware publications for my CyberCorps and Advanced Malware Forensics research project. Based on the paper "Prudent Practices for Designing Malware Experiments," I re-performed the original experiment to determine if proper sampling, analysis, and safety measures improved since 2012.

I plan to continue developing this paper in the hope of submitting it to a conference sometime in Fall 2020.

Forensics Curriculum Development

August 2019

In RIT's Computer System Forensics class, students learn necessary incident response procedures and methods to uncover and investigate computer users' activities. While the course is well-designed and the concepts presented are highly applicable, assignments and labs can grow a bit stale.

Such was the case for Spring 2019's memory acquisition and analysis lab. I rewrote this lab to provide a more reliable Volatility Framework experience.

My updated lab blog post is available below.

Volatility Lab

RITSEC Demo Writeups

May 2019

I wrote weekly CTF challenge solutions for the semester-long Capture the Flag challenge by RITSEC. The goal is for my solutions to give insight into the strategy and reasoning of a CTF. I took first place during the semester I wrote these solutions, and I hope that my experience can help others achieve this too.

I do this because as a freshman, I did not have experience with these tools and didn't know who to ask for help. I hope my solutions will help new freshmen get started with the fun of CTFs.

Rogue Network Devices

December 2018

To expand on the topics covered in my Network Services class for an Honors project, I identified common security vulnerabilities and oversights that would allow an attacker to control a network.

Then, I created security vulnerabilities and rogue devices on a network, determined how to detect and remove them effectively, and learned about technologies to increase network security. I presented the project to my class on our free lab day at the end of fall 2018.
